Why Are We Having This Conversation?

You are entering academia at the exact moment when the norms around AI are being written. As PhD students, you are simultaneously:

  • Students subject to academic integrity policies
  • Researchers who may use AI as a tool in your work
  • Teachers who will need to set norms for your own students
  • Emerging scholars who will publish under journal policies that are changing rapidly

The rules are unsettled. The detection tools are flawed. The stakes are your career. You need a framework, not just a list of dos and don'ts.

Four Distinct Conversations

AI in academia is not one issue. It is at least four:

1. AI as a Writing Tool - drafting, editing, polishing prose

2. AI as a Research Tool - literature discovery, data analysis, coding

3. AI and Academic Integrity - detection, disclosure, institutional policy

4. AI as an Object of Study - what AI means for IR, governance, power

Each has different ethical considerations. Conflating them leads to confused policy and confused practice.

The PhD-Specific Problem

A PhD certifies that you can construct a sustained, original argument across 80,000 words. Anything that short-circuits that development is ultimately self-defeating, even if the output looks polished.

The slow, painful process of writing is where conceptual clarity develops. Using AI to skip that process is like hiring someone to do your physiotherapy exercises - the report might say you completed the programme, but your knee still does not work.

You are not producing a document. You are building a capability. The document is evidence that the capability exists.

AI as a Research Tool

AI can now assist with:

  • Literature discovery and summarisation
  • Coding qualitative data
  • Identifying patterns in large datasets
  • Translating sources
  • Generating interview transcripts

The Epistemological Problem

Positivist: Is AI-assisted analysis replicable and auditable?

Interpretivist: You cannot outsource Verstehen to a language model. Meaning-making requires human judgement.

Critical: Who built these tools? On what data? Embedding whose assumptions?

The Hallucination Problem

AI fabricates confidently.

Large language models generate plausible-sounding text that may be factually wrong. In academic contexts, this is catastrophic:

Hallucinated citations: AI will invent references - real author names, real journal titles, fabricated articles that do not exist. A 2025 analysis found over 100 hallucinated citations in papers accepted at a top ML conference.

Fabricated claims: AI will generate statistical findings, historical dates, and theoretical positions that sound authoritative but are wrong.

If you use AI and do not verify every claim and every citation against the actual source, you are putting your name on someone else's lies.

How AI Detection Works

The Technical Reality

AI detectors (Turnitin, GPTZero, Originality.ai) work by measuring two properties of text:

Perplexity: How predictable is each word given the words before it? AI text tends to choose the most statistically likely next word, making it low-perplexity (smooth, predictable).

Burstiness: How much does sentence length and complexity vary? Human writing is "bursty" - mixing short and long sentences unpredictably. AI text is more uniform.

These tools produce a probability score, not a verdict. There is no digital fingerprint. There is no way to definitively prove a paragraph was written by AI.

Turnitin's AI Detection

What the Evidence Shows

  • Turnitin claims it catches ~85% of unmodified AI text with a false positive rate "below 1%"
  • Independent testing tells a different story. A 2026 study reported a mean false positive rate of 61.3% for non-native English writing samples
  • The Stanford HAI study (2023) found AI detectors unanimously misidentified 19% of TOEFL essays written by non-native speakers as AI-generated
  • Turnitin itself disputed the bias claim in a 2024 study but the methodological debate continues
  • Curtin University (Australia) disabled Turnitin's AI detection entirely from January 2026, calling it unreliable

Formal academic writing, non-native English prose, and heavily edited text all trigger false positives at elevated rates.

The Fundamental Problem with Detection

Detectors don't identify AI. They identify "text that looks like text they think AI would write." Those are two very different things.
- Reddit user, r/Turnitinservicehelp, February 2026

As researchers trained in methods, you should recognise this immediately:

  • A probabilistic score is not evidence of misconduct
  • The tool has known biases against specific populations (L2 writers)
  • The tool cannot distinguish between AI-generated and AI-polished text
  • Paraphrased or lightly edited AI text often evades detection entirely

Treating a Turnitin AI score as proof is roughly equivalent to treating a polygraph result as proof. It is probabilistic, it is biased, and it is not something you want your career to hinge on.

Our Institutional Reality

What You Are Currently Subject To

Our department currently relies on Turnitin with AI detection enabled. This is the tool that has documented false positive issues, particularly for non-native English speakers - which describes a significant portion of our student body at EMU.

The practical implications:

  • You may be flagged even if you did not use AI
  • The flag is a probability score, not proof
  • What happens next depends on whether your institution treats the score as a smoke alarm or a verdict
  • You need to be prepared to demonstrate your writing process

The Trend

Universities are beginning to shift from "prove you didn't use AI" toward process-based assessment: grading drafts, oral defences, in-class checkpoints.

The COPE Position

The Committee on Publication Ethics (COPE) has established the baseline that most publishers follow:

1. AI tools cannot be listed as authors because they cannot take responsibility for the work

2. Authors must disclose AI use transparently

3. Authors are fully accountable for accuracy, originality, and integrity of all content - including any AI-assisted portions

4. Peer reviewers should not upload manuscripts to AI tools (confidentiality)

COPE's 2025 focus session identified a growing gap between stated policies and actual practice - many researchers are using AI but not disclosing it.

How to Disclose: A Template

When you use AI in your research or writing, disclose clearly. A safe template:

"The author(s) used [tool name, e.g., ChatGPT, GPT-4, Claude] for [specific task, e.g., language editing of the discussion section / literature search assistance / initial coding of interview transcripts]. All output was reviewed, verified, and revised by the author(s), who take full responsibility for the final manuscript."

Key elements:

  • Name the specific tool and version if known
  • State the specific task it performed
  • Confirm human review of all output
  • Accept responsibility for the final product

Place the disclosure where your target journal requires it - Methods, Acknowledgements, or submission form. Check before submitting.

Four Principles for Defensible AI Use

1. Transparency

Whatever you use AI for, document and disclose it. This protects you and builds trust. Non-disclosure is increasingly treated as misconduct.

2. The Value-Added Test

If AI did the work, what did you contribute? If you cannot articulate your intellectual contribution clearly, you have gone too far.

3. The Competence Principle

Do not use AI to do something you cannot evaluate. If you cannot assess whether an AI-generated analysis is correct, you should not be using it for that purpose.

4. The Development Principle

As a PhD student, your goal is not just to produce outputs but to build capabilities. Using AI in ways that prevent skill development is self-sabotaging.

The Research Assistant Analogy

Think of AI the way you think about a research assistant:

Acceptable: An RA pulls sources, cleans data, formats references, transcribes interviews. You acknowledge their contribution.

Not acceptable: An RA writes your theoretical argument, constructs your analysis, or develops your conclusions - and you put your name on it.

AI sits on this same spectrum. The question is always: where on the spectrum is my use, and can I defend it?

The right frame is not "will I get caught?" but "can I defend what I did to my supervisor, my examiners, and a journal editor?"

Protecting Yourself from False Positives

Practical Steps Every PhD Student Should Take

  • Keep all drafts. Save every version of your chapters. Use Google Docs (which has version history) or regularly save dated copies. This creates an evidence trail of your writing process.
  • Keep your notes and outlines. Handwritten notes, mind maps, annotated PDFs - anything that shows your thinking developed before the text appeared.
  • Use track changes. When revising, keep tracked changes visible in at least one saved version.
  • Document your AI use. If you use AI for any purpose, keep a log: date, tool, prompt, what you did with the output.
  • Be ready for the conversation. If flagged, you need to be able to walk someone through your argument, your sources, and your reasoning. If you wrote it, this should be easy.

The best protection against a false positive is a documented writing process that makes the accusation implausible.

What AI Changes About Your Value as a Scholar

If AI can produce competent literature reviews, data summaries, and polished prose, then what makes a scholar valuable shifts toward:

  • Original theorising - AI cannot generate new theoretical frameworks
  • Creative research design - AI cannot design a study that asks an interesting question in a novel way
  • Critical judgement - AI cannot evaluate whether an argument is persuasive, an interpretation is defensible, or a finding is significant
  • Deep contextual knowledge - AI cannot do fieldwork, build relationships, or read an archive with sensitivity to what matters
  • Ethical reasoning - AI cannot navigate the political and moral dimensions of research

Students who develop these skills will be more valuable, not less. The premium on genuine expertise rises as the cost of generic competence falls to zero.

Grey Areas for Discussion

Where Do You Draw the Line?

Scenario 1: You use AI to translate a Turkish-language primary source into English for your dissertation. You verify the translation against your own knowledge. Disclose? Acceptable?

Scenario 2: You paste your draft literature review into ChatGPT and ask "what am I missing?" It suggests three additional scholars. You read their work and add them. Disclose?

Scenario 3: You use AI to generate an initial coding scheme for 50 interview transcripts. You then revise the codes extensively based on your reading. Disclose? How much revision makes it "yours"?

Scenario 4: You ask AI to "improve the clarity" of your theoretical argument section. It reorganises your paragraphs and rewrites several sentences. The ideas are yours but many of the words are not. Acceptable?

What Actually Gets You in Trouble

Real Patterns from Integrity Committees

The hallucinated reference list: A research team used ChatGPT to draft an introduction. 12 of 43 references did not exist - plausible combinations of real author names and real journals. Two reviewers flagged it. Rejection + formal warning + permanent flag on the author's submission record.

The generic discussion: A reviewer returned a manuscript with one comment: "This reads like it was generated by a language model. Every paragraph makes broad claims without connecting to the specific results." Two additional revision rounds before eventual publication.

The missing disclosure: AI use discovered post-publication (whistleblower report or editor cross-check). Consequence: published correction or retraction. Reputational damage is permanent.

AI as an IR Question

As IR scholars, you should also be thinking about AI as a phenomenon within your field:

  • Power and governance: Who controls AI development? What are the geopolitical implications of AI concentration in a handful of US and Chinese firms?
  • Security: How does AI change warfare, surveillance, and deterrence? What happens to strategic stability when decision-making is automated?
  • Political economy: How does AI reshape global labour markets, trade, and development?
  • Norms and institutions: How are international norms around AI governance being constructed? Who is included and excluded?
  • Knowledge production: What does it mean for the discipline when the tools we use to study the world are themselves reshaping the world?

Some of you may end up writing dissertations about these very questions.

Key Takeaways

1. AI detection tools are unreliable and biased. Do not assume a Turnitin score is a verdict. Protect yourself with documented writing processes.

2. Every major publisher now requires AI disclosure. Non-disclosure is treated as misconduct. Always disclose, always be specific.

3. The right question is not "will I get caught?" but "can I defend what I did?" Apply the value-added test: if you cannot articulate your intellectual contribution, you have gone too far.

4. A PhD builds capabilities, not just documents. Using AI in ways that prevent you from developing core scholarly skills is self-defeating even when it is not dishonest.

5. The policies are evolving. Stay informed. Check your target journal's specific guidelines before every submission. Do not assume all publishers are the same.

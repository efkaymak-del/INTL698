Why Professional Activities Matter

Your dissertation is necessary but not sufficient. An academic career is built on visibility, relationships, and reputation — and these are built through professional activities outside the dissertation itself.

Conferences — where your work gets tested in public for the first time

Networking — how opportunities (jobs, collaborations, invitations) actually circulate

Your profile — how people find you when they are looking for expertise, reviewers, or collaborators

Methods training — how you build skills your programme may not offer

None of this is optional if you want an academic career. The earlier you start, the less daunting it becomes.

What Conferences Actually Do For You

The presentation itself is often the least valuable part. The real returns are:

  • Feedback on work in progress — a discussant or audience member spots a flaw or suggests a source you missed. This improves your dissertation chapter before it becomes permanent.
  • Discovering adjacent work — the panel you attend where someone is working on the same problem from a different angle. This shapes your literature review and theoretical positioning.
  • Being seen — the senior scholar who hears your question during Q&A, the editor who sees your abstract in the programme. Visibility compounds over time.
  • Professional socialisation — you learn how the discipline works by watching it work. How people present, how they handle tough questions, how panels are organised.
Conferences are networking events disguised as intellectual events. The hallway conversations matter as much as the panels.

The Money Problem

Being Honest About Costs

A typical in-person conference costs:

Registration: EUR 150-400 (often reduced for PhD students)

Flights: EUR 200-800 depending on destination

Accommodation: EUR 300-600 for 3-4 nights

Meals and transport: EUR 100-200

Total per conference: EUR 750-2,000

For PhD students at EMU, attending ISA in North America can easily cost EUR 1,500+. That is not a trivial expense.

The Strategic Calculation

One well-chosen conference where you prepare thoroughly, present strong work, and network deliberately is worth more than three where you just show up. Budget your time and money strategically.

Funding Your Conference Attendance

A Checklist of Sources

  • ECPR grants — up to GBP 500 for travel/accommodation. You are eligible as EMU students. Apply via ecpr.eu/Funding
  • Conference fee waivers — many conferences offer reduced/waived fees for students from developing countries or low-income backgrounds. Always ask.
  • ISA travel grants — ISA offers travel grants for scholars from the Global South and for first-time presenters
  • IPSA travel support — available for World Congress participants from underrepresented regions
  • BISA bursaries — BISA offers bursaries for postgraduate attendees, especially first-time presenters
  • EISA fee waivers — available for scholars from institutions with limited funding
  • Your department/university — ask whether EMU has a travel fund for PhD student conference presentations. If not, advocate for one.
  • External funding bodies — TUBiTAK (Turkey), national research councils, private foundations

Key rule: Never assume you cannot attend because of cost without first checking every available funding source. Always apply. The worst they can say is no.

Methods Schools and Summer Schools

Invest in skills your programme may not teach

ECPR Methods School — runs alongside the General Conference. Courses on QCA, process tracing, discourse analysis, mixed methods, statistics. Krakow, September 2026. Funding available for EMU students.

ECPR Summer School on Concepts and Methods — focused thematic schools (e.g., far-right politics methods). Application-based.

These programmes build skills, but they also build networks. The people in your methods school cohort become your peer group for years to come.

Networking Without Pain

You do not have to be extroverted. You have to be prepared.

  • Before the conference: Read the programme. Identify 3-5 people whose work connects to yours. Read their recent work. Prepare one thoughtful question or comment for each.
  • Your elevator pitch: Prepare a two-sentence description of your research. Practice it until it feels natural. "I'm working on X because Y is puzzling. I'm looking at it through Z."
  • During panels: Ask good questions. This is the single best networking strategy. A smart question makes you memorable. It costs nothing and requires no small talk.
  • Coffee breaks and dinners: Sit with people you do not know. Introduce yourself simply. Ask what they are working on. People love talking about their research.
  • After the conference: Send a brief email within a week to anyone you had a meaningful conversation with. "It was good to meet you at X. I found your comments on Y very helpful. I would love to stay in touch."
Networking is not self-promotion. It is building a community of people who know what you do and what you care about.

Beyond Conferences: The Full Picture

Professional activities you should be building into your routine

  • Peer reviewing — when invited to review for a journal, say yes (if competent). It builds your understanding of what editors look for, improves your own writing, and puts you in the network. You will start being invited after your first publication or conference presentation.
  • Organising panels and workshops — proposing a panel at ISA or ECPR is a leadership signal and a way to build a group around a topic you care about. You do not have to be senior to do this.
  • Academic social media — Bluesky (increasingly replacing Twitter for academics), LinkedIn for professional updates. Share your work, engage with others' work, signal what you are interested in. Keep it professional.
  • Policy engagement — if your work has policy relevance, write a short accessible piece for a policy blog (The Loop at ECPR, The Monkey Cage, Duck of Minerva, E-IR). This builds public visibility.
  • Teaching portfolio — if you teach, document it. Many hiring committees want evidence of teaching capacity alongside research.

Building Your Academic Profile

If people cannot find you online, you do not exist

Google Scholar profile — free, takes 5 minutes to set up, automatically tracks your citations. Do this today if you have not already.

ORCID — a persistent digital identifier that links all your work across publishers and institutions. Required by many journals. Takes 2 minutes: orcid.org

Institutional page — make sure your EMU department page is up to date with your research interests, current project, and contact information.

Personal academic website — a simple one-page site with your bio, research description, CV, and any publications. GitHub Pages (which you already know how to use) works perfectly for this. Free.

Academia.edu / ResearchGate — upload your papers and conference presentations. These platforms have large user bases and drive discovery.

When a journal editor is looking for a reviewer, or a conference organiser is building a panel, they search online. Make yourself findable.

Common Mistakes

  • Waiting until your work is "ready" — conferences are for work in progress. If your paper is finished and polished, it should be at a journal, not a conference. Present early, get feedback, improve.
  • Treating conferences as tourism — attending panels passively, not talking to anyone, seeing the city. You are investing time and money. Extract maximum value.
  • Presenting the same paper everywhere — diminishing returns. Each conference should feature a different paper or a significantly revised version.
  • Ignoring virtual options — the ISA Virtual Conference (July 2026) has the same intellectual standards as the in-person convention at a fraction of the cost. There is no shame in presenting virtually.
  • Not following up — the connection you made at a conference evaporates if you do not follow up with an email within a week. This is where most PhD students fail.
  • Neglecting your online presence — no Google Scholar profile, no website, no ORCID. You are invisible to anyone who does not already know you personally.
